# Nagoya Protocol and ABS regulation

As a life science researcher in Sweden, if your work involves the utilization of genetic resources or traditional knowledge from outside of Sweden, you may need to adhere to the **Nagoya Protocol** and the **EU Access and Benefit-Sharing (ABS) Regulation (EU) No 511/2014**. 

## What is it?
The Nagoya Protocol is a supplementary agreement to the UN Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD). Its core objective is **Access and Benefit-Sharing (ABS)**: ensuring that the benefits arising from the utilization of genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge are shared fairly and equitably with the providing country.

* **Genetic Resources:** Any material of plant, animal, microbial, or other origin containing functional units of heredity, or derivatives (e.g. RNA, proteins, viral materials, or specific biochemical compounds).
* **Utilisation:** Conducting research and development on the genetic and/or biochemical composition of these resources, including through biotechnology. This applies to both basic and applied research.

In Sweden, the EU ABS Regulation is fully legally binding. Failing to comply can lead to serious legal and academic consequences. The **Swedish Environmental Protection Agency (*Naturvårdsverket*)** is the supervisory authority responsible for checking compliance, issuing guidance, and monitoring researchers in Sweden.

## How to comply

Swedish researchers have a strict obligation to exercise ***due diligence***, meaning that you must investigate and ensure that you have aquired the genetic resource in accordance with the legislation of the provider country. Hence, if your research falls within the scope of the Nagoya Protocol, you must obtain the necessary permits from the provider country. The names and numbers of  permits may vary, but often a Prior Informed Consent (PIC), and Mutually Agreed Terms (MAT) are required. 

Reporting is done via EU's digital tool, DECLARE. Always contact your university's legal or research support office before acquiring material. Most likely they have both guidance as well as an account in the tool, as these typically is obtained at an organisational level.

You are required to submit a due diligence declaration via DECLARE at two points in time:
1.  **Research Phase:** After the first installment of research funding is received, but no later than the submission of the final project report.
2.  **Product Development Phase:** At the final stage of developing a product derived from the genetic resource.

From a data management perspective, compliance requires strict record-keeping:
 * You must preserve all documentation (permits, agreements, declaration receipts) for **20 years** after the period of utilization ends.
* Ensure your data workflows track the exact origin, sample IDs, and associated permits.
* When publishing, you may be required to reference the Internationally Recognized Certificate of Compliance (IRCC) number.
